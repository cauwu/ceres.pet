APPENDIX 3

2nd Gateway Workshops

Tech Lens Workshop

Ceres Miller s1969357

Assessing if objects meant to generate intrigue in the people that interact with them adds value to those peoples’ experiences learning about those objects.

The people I’m using to assess will be people who have expressed interest in wanting to learn about digital electronics. I will conduct the workshop in around 20 minutes in a quiet environment, with people I’ve arranged to meet for it. In the workshop I will give people these objects to look at and play with while I explain how they work and take their questions throughout. I will note down their reactions and thoughts as the workshop is going on, and at the end, ask them verbally:

  • If they found these objects useful to have,
  • What features about the objects they found useful,
  • If they enjoyed the experience and if the objects helped that enjoyment.

The objects I have made for this workshop:

  • A model in brass wire of an AND logic gate,
  • An embroidered model of an XOR logic gate,
  • Teaching materials, cut into squares approx 100mm x 100mm, mounted on 3mm foam board.

Participants, setting

The ideal participants I’m looking at for this test, and also subsequent tests in this series:

  • Have little/no knowledge of electronics or digital logic
  • Have expressed a desire to want to learn about electronics or digital logic (“How computers work”)
  • Are adults
  • Have had trouble understanding electronics or digital logic, or not attempted to learn for perceived difficulty

I will conduct workshops with people I know previously, in a comfortable and quiet setting.

If I can I may also conduct workshops with groups of people I can be introduced to. This could be in a studio environment.

Beginning a workshop I will try to get people comfortable, eg, providing tea and biscuits, what my testing is for, if in a studio making sure everyone knows where the toilets and exits are.

Workshop

The teaching material is a very short introduction to binary logic in a 4-bit adder. It demonstrates how electrical signals become a simple mathematical operation.

First when I show people the models of the AND and XOR logic gates I will tell them what they are and give them some time to interact with them to begin with. I then inform people that they can continue to play with the models while I explain them, and that they can stop me to ask questions at any point or give their thoughts. Then using the teaching materials I will explain how they work;

  • First explaining that digital logic is electrical signals of HIGH and LOW one at a time, and that binary numbers are those electrical signals in sequences.
  • How to read binary numbers.
  • What a logic gate is using the examples of AND, XOR and OR.
  • Explaining how the physical electronics work, like transistors and resistors, to make the AND and XOR gates work.
  • That lots of logic gates together can make complex digital logic, with the example of a half adder and a full adder.
  • Stringing a half adder and 3 adders together creates a machine that can add two 4-bit numbers together.
  • That even more complex operations can be done in the same way, which is the basis for computers.

I then ask people if they understand what was explained to them and note their response.

Finally ask them their opinions:

  • If they found the objects useful to have while trying to understand what was being explained,
  • If what the objects are was useful or not to their learning and what about the objects helped or didn’t,
  • And if they enjoyed the experience and if the objects affected that enjoyment.

Method of processing results

I have no way of collecting results quantitatively and I rely on chatting and noting down reactions during and after a workshop to make up my research on how people respond to what I’ve made.

I use these qualitative results in a qualitative way. I take what people have suggested, what they liked, what they wanted to see, and whether or not they felt like what they were doing was useful and I use that to reflect on what I’ve done and decide what next needs to be done.

Parse

The two workshop sessions I did both went very well and my participants said they enjoyed the experience and retained information, but major points they told me about learning was that the objects were difficult to parse and as far as teaching how logic gates worked, diagrams on paper and truth tables over physical models should be preferred. A participant also said that they were less motivated to learn and more interested in the objects. Another said that the education was lacking a strong narrative—why should someone learn this? What are these logic gates used for? I had run the workshop in the context of a 4-bit adder, but nobody knew what that was before my explanation. All of this would imply that although my objects made for an enjoyable learning experience that worked on my participants, they themselves say that using these objects is not a good way to learn.

My participants had asked for more interaction in these objects. They wanted them to work, to do something, to build something with them; like putting them together like building blocks to make something tangible. This suggests they wanted something out of my work that they were not getting. They found the teaching material being on slates made it much more engaging, but this isn’t that useful to my project.

Takeaways

People are very interested in the way that these models are presented and would like to see me do more with them. Interactions, use, touch

They would like me to make sure that as I’m teaching I stay closely in narrative to what I’m explaining (A 4-bit adder is a tiny section of a computer)

APPENDIX 5

Social Lens Workshop

Ceres Miller s1969357

Seeing what comes out of making digital electronics with others who don’t know much about them.

People who have done the previous workshop with me get asked to make things with me based on what they’ve learned. The purpose of the workshop is to see what comes out of doing this in terms of physical artefacts, and if working with others is valuable. Specifically I want to know if what people make, when those objects are presented as having been made by people who didn’t know much about digital electronics, they make people feel differently about digital electronics.

The workshop takes around an hour. People sit down with lots of easy and quick to use material for making electronics; foamboard, conductive ink, copper tape, components (resistors, transistors, batteries, leds), hot glue, and pens. Participants will also be given foamboard shapes, I have made cubes, to use as something to get people thinking by narrowing down possibilities.

Participants, setting

Participants were the same people who had done the previous workshop.

For what categories I was looking for; people who didn’t know much about electronics or digital electronics, but wanted to learn.

This workshop is done with me constantly participating in it alongside others. A comfortable space for making things with a small amount of people.

Workshop

Materials will all be laid out when we start.

Using foamboard shapes, I’ll start making first, anything that comes to mind around using the materials I have to hand. I’ll talk constantly about what I’m doing to give the participants an idea of what they’re meant to be doing and thinking about too.

I’ll talk them through any ideas they have. Once they settle on something, I’ll help them figure out how it will work, and then help them build it.

The whole time I’ll be noting down their experiences, reactions, and statements. I’m looking for their opinions about the experience, problems they have with it, things they want out of it, and if they’re enjoying what they’re doing/making.

Materials used

Foamboard cubes used as a starting point

At the end of the hour we’ll look at what we did, and I’ll ask the participants questions:

  • Do you feel like physical making made you feel different now about digital electronics than before?
  • Would you be willing to do this kind of thing on your own, to make things for your own uses?
  • Did you enjoy the experience?

Method of Processing Results

I took participants' responses and got their opinion on each point by reading their tone, excitement, and statements, both during and after the workshop. Any frustration and problems encountered during the workshop I noted.

I worked out whether the question I asked can be affirmed or not based on positive or negative tone or content in responses. Using this I was able to get answers to my questions that I could turn into reflections and my next actions.

Raw notes and responses, Social Lens Workshop

i didn't know how to introduce getting someone creative like this.

i set out all the materials and the foamboard cubes,
and reintroduced the area of investigation of my project, nonstandard ways of building digital electronics, with more context about what we're doing in this workshop; that i'm trying to get people to be creative with building these electronics.

we started slowly, i tried to keep constant communication about what i was doing and suggestions about what could be done. i was making alongside the other person and said that as i start doing something and showing what i was doing, maybe ideas would come to them for what they could make.

we started verbally ideating as we were playing with materials. as i was talking about how i ideate; i gave the example of the foamboard cubes, that you could arrange them in different ways to get different results. immediately the other person had the idea of making a maze of traces that if you hold it at certain points which close a circuit using your fingers, it would power two pieces of copper tape that could be used to send a signal to something else; like a kind of key.

we talked quickly about how we would make it; and since this person was unsure about electronics, i set up an example of a circuit using a breadboard, an led, a battery, a button, and jumper cables. i explained how it all worked, and the person then took it apart and reassembled it. they got me to help them make it work, and then they began working on their puzzle-key-cube.

as they worked we passed it back and forth making sure that it was all working. once it was made we fixed problems with it and it worked in the end.

the other person said they had a lot of fun doing this and genuinely enjoyed the experience. they were very proud of what they had made.

Parse

Although the workshop went really well and my participant enjoyed it; “it gave a practicality and simplicity that I didn’t think would be workable for a know-nothing like me”, they did say that it “didn’t change their perception of electronics more generally”. I’m trying to look at very complicated systems in a short time, low-level computer logic, and this little snapshot doesn’t provide greater context to the electronics we use day-to-day.

Having both me there and working with physical materials was good for learning, and my participant was able to learn the concepts I was teaching well. Again, knowing about learning isn’t that useful for my project.

My participant said it would be fun to do more making on their own, but they have no uses for the kinds of things we were doing in this workshop, and they would prefer someone like me to be around to help them through it. The workshop didn’t convince them to make on their own.

What was produced from the workshop

Takeaways

I’m doing a lot of work and research into learning when learning isn’t the focus of my project. I probably need to stop looking at learning as a theme.

I’m focussing a lot on logic gates when I should be exploring a lot of other things—I’ve only got so much time, so I should really broaden what I’m looking at quickly.

I didn’t show something in this workshop that was applicable to people’s lives in terms of making. Having workshops making something or using materials that give participants broad possibilities for their own uses would probably be most useful for them.

It was really nice to see what other people can come up with, and importantly, what they respond well to in regards to making things. Making with others should probably be an important part of my project if the point is to find the value of nonstandard ways of making digital electronics.
