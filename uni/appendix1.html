Mind’s Eye Camera Creativity Workshop

30/04/23 Ceres Miller

Overview

I found 7 participants for this workshop. It was conducted over 15 minutes in a cafe.

I gave paper and pens out to everyone and asked them to sketch or write down ways that they could think of to change the camera in any way.

Detail

To help facilitate, I explained how I had made the camera, the significance of the brass and PLA, and how the housing was so open to help make it easier to add and change parts. I explained what the camera could currently do, that is take pictures and video with the quality of a webcam, and be plugged into a TV and not much else. However, I said that the camera has the potential to do more than a retail digital camera can; like streaming video wirelessly or adding custom filters and overlays, it just needs you to add a button for it. I suggested a modification, like a battery. This was enough to get people to begin ideating.

Research data collection

Throughout the workshop I noted down points that people were sharing, and I also noted down their reactions, such as excitement or touching. I also gathered the papers that they had.

Research data processing

To quantify the data, I looked at each point or reaction I had recorded and considered whether it exhibited things I was looking for; like creativity, excitement, or enthusiasm, or things I was looking to avoid; such as confusion or apprehension. I tallied up each of these points to get a final outcome.

My conclusions also take note of specific things people had said or done that stood out to me. This combined with the tally count will inform how I use the results of my research.

Processed Data

I have split the responses into categories;

  • Positive: Enthusiasm, compliment, simile (eg, “looks like…”), excitement
  • Negative: Confusion, apprehension, self-doubt
  • Neutral: eg, “Technical”, “All our suggestions are just guns”
  • Modifications

Modifications I consider a good thing, as a demonstration of creativity. I have organised modifications as a separate kind of point to avoid skewing the positive tally too much.

Neutral is a category for responses that don’t appear to be positive or negative, or the mood of the response would vary person to person.

I have considered simile as positive because it shows imaginative thinking as a result of viewing the camera. I have considered the “traffic cops” part a bad thing however, as I don’t want the camera to be considered sinister. I didn’t like that it looks so much like a gun, but I am willing to accept it because it communicates how to hold it and suggests that the cable box must be removable. But I don’t like that it looks like a cop’s camera.

Tallies

  • Positive points: 11
  • Negative points: 4
  • Neutral points: 2
  • Modifications: 51

I was surprised that a participant did not recognise it as a camera until told. I considered that a bad thing, as the shape may be confusing and complicated where I had imagined each element such as the lens piece and buttons being instantly parseable.

“Everything spilling on it” suggests a kind of fragility that I had tried to avoid by using brass and wrapping the plates around the camera somewhat.

I liked that it was recognised as not being able to take good pictures. I don’t want people to think the camera is meant to be top of the line, rather it is a toy and a tool.

I liked that participants considered it fashionable. I was worried that it looked uneven, inconsistent, cobbled together, too much like a rough development prototype.

“Loads of space to put things in” was exactly what I was going for.

Conclusions

Making the camera look more like a camera is a priority. This might be achieved by screwing it into a tripod mount. Having the camera be instantly parseable is important as communicating purpose, and therefore what each of the components might do, is essential to avoid apprehension to learning.

Making it look less like a traffic cop’s speed camera might involve making the lens piece a different shape, perhaps without a flat face on the front.

People obviously are able to think creatively when looking at the camera, and the materials and openness of it I think are the reason. Being able straight through the camera helps when planning changes, someone is able to see exactly where they might drill a hole in the housing to put something new. The camera looking like every part inside of it can be shifted around I think also helps with planning new things.
